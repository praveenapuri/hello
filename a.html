Phase 1 — Design the Information Architecture 1) Define the scope and sources Inventory repositories: list current locations (SharePoint sites, Teams files, OneDrive, shared drives, email archives, third-party DMS). Classify policy types: e.g., Corporate, HR, Security, Privacy, IT, Finance, Legal, Country/Region-specific. Identify authoritative owners: Policy Authors, Approvers, Reviewers, Records/Compliance officers. Deliverable: Source inventory and ownership matrix. 2) Metadata & Content Types (the foundation) Create reusable Content Types (via the SharePoint Content Type Gallery) so policies are consistently tagged across the org. Example schema: Site Columns (examples) Policy ID (Single line text) – e.g., “HR-TR-001” Policy Title (Single line text) Policy Type (Choice) – Corporate | HR | IT | Legal | Security | Privacy | Finance | Regional | Other Function / Department (Managed Metadata or Choice) Jurisdiction (Managed Metadata: Global, US, EU, APAC, etc.) Effective Date (Date) Review Cycle (Months) (Number) Next Review Date (Calculated from Effective Date + Review Cycle) Owner (Person or Group) Approver (Person or Group) Status (Choice) – Draft | In Review | Approved | Published | Retired Supersedes Policy (Lookup to same library by Policy ID) Superseded By (Lookup to same library by Policy ID) Version Label (Single line text — e.g., “v2.1”) Data Category (Choice) – General | Confidential | Highly Confidential Keywords (Managed Metadata) Audience Tags (Managed Metadata) – e.g., “Managers”, “All Employees”, “Vendors” Regulatory Mapping (Managed Metadata) – e.g., SOX, HIPAA, GDPR Article 32 Document Type (Choice) – Policy | Standard | Procedure | Guideline | Form Content Types Policy (Master) – includes all columns. Optional specialized types: HR Policy, IT Policy, etc. (inherit from Policy and add domain-specific fields). Why this matters: Strong metadata is what will make the AI bot far more accurate in policy applicability and conflict detection. 3) Library & Site structure Create a dedicated SharePoint site: Policy Center (communication site recommended for broad read access). Create a single Policy Library (or a very small number, e.g., Policies, Standards & Procedures if volume is massive). Enable: Versioning (Major/minor as needed; recommend Major versions for published, minor during drafting in a separate workspace, see governance) Require Check-out (optional; helps control edits) Unique Content Types (enable management of content types) Views: “Published Policies” – filter Status = Published “By Department” – grouped by Function / Department “By Jurisdiction” “By Next Review Date” (ascending) “Recently Updated” Permissions model Policy Center site: Visitors: Everyone except external users (Read) Members: Policy Editors (Contribute) Owners: Policy Governance Team (Full Control) Drafting workspace (optional but recommended): A separate Team/SharePoint site for drafting with restricted access. Use Power Automate or Publishing flows to promote to the Policy Center upon approval. 4) Governance & Compliance Approval workflow: Draft → Review → Approver sign-off → Publish. Use Power Automate with Planner tasks/Approvals or SharePoint approval. Retention & Records: Use Microsoft Purview retention labels: e.g., “Policy — Retain 7 years” after superseded/retired. Configure auto-apply based on the Document Type = Policy. Watermarking/Headers (optional): Use Word templates with headers/footers such as “Controlled Document”, classification label, version. Change log: Encourage maintainers to summarize changes in the Document Information Panel or maintain a Change Summary column or use the Word document’s change history. Deliverables: IA spec (columns, content types), permission matrix, retention plan, approval workflow design. Phase 4 — Build the AI Bot You have two recommended pathways, depending on how much control and sophistication you want: Option A — Copilot Studio (no/low-code, fastest path) Great for natural language Q&A over SharePoint content with RAG (retrieval-augmented generation) and secure-by-default permissions. Steps Create a Copilot Studio bot Go to Microsoft Copilot Studio → Create a copilot. Name: “Policy Advisor”. Add data sources Add a SharePoint data connection to the Policy Center library. Configure the Indexing to include file content and metadata. Add specific URLs or use the entire library. Define Topics & Prompts Topics: “Which policy applies?” Prompt example to the model: Given the user’s question, list the policies that most likely apply. Explain why each policy is relevant, referencing the metadata (Policy Type, Jurisdiction, Function) and quoting specific clauses with citations and links. If no policy is relevant, ask clarifying questions. “Conflict detection & alignment” Prompt to model: Compare the most relevant policies. If there are contradictions or overlapping clauses, highlight them. Indicate priority based on Jurisdiction and Status (Published vs Draft), and whether any policy supersedes another. Propose a reconciliation summary. Add clarifying questions (e.g., “What is your department?”, “Which country/region?”, “Employee or contractor?”, “Is it a travel or expense scenario?”). Guardrails & grounding Enable Citations and require the bot to cite policy links and the specific clause sections. Configure Fallback prompts: if confidence is low, suggest reaching out to the policy owner. Entities & Variables Create entities like Jurisdiction, Department, EmployeeType for better routing and filtering. Business logic Add filter rules based on metadata: If Department=HR → prefer HR policies first. If Jurisdiction=EU → prefer EU/Global over US. If Status ≠ Published → deprioritize unless user is an Editor. Publish & Channels Publish internally. Add the bot to Teams and SharePoint as a web part on the Policy Center home page. Analytics Use Copilot Studio analytics to see unresolved queries, improve topics, and refine prompts. Pros: Fast, native auth/permissions, low-code. Cons: Conflict detection is prompt/heuristic-based; deep logic may require extension. Option B — Azure OpenAI + Azure AI Search (vector/RAG) + Graph connectors (more control, advanced logic) Best if you want a robust conflict detection and policy-rule reasoning pipeline. Architecture Data: SharePoint Policy Library (+ optional external repositories via Graph connectors). Ingestion: Use an indexing pipeline to chunk documents (e.g., 512–2000 tokens), store embeddings (Azure OpenAI text-embedding-3-large or equivalent) and metadata. Add Azure AI Search with vector + keyword indexes. RAG Orchestration: User question → classify entities (Jurisdiction, Department) → construct search filters → retrieve top-k chunks → synthesize answer with citations. Conflict detection module: Step 1: Policy Candidate Selection – Retrieve top policies based on filters + semantic similarity. Step 2: Clause Extraction – Extract relevant clauses from each candidate (chunk-level). Step 3: Rule Normalization – Convert natural language clauses into structured rule statements (e.g., subject, action, condition, threshold, exceptions). Step 4: Conflict Analysis – Compare rule statements for: Direct contradiction (e.g., “require” vs “prohibit” under same conditions), Threshold mismatches (e.g., “$500 limit” vs “$800 limit”), Jurisdiction precedence (e.g., Country policy overrides Global), Version precedence (Published, Effective Date, Supersedes). Step 5: Prioritization – Determine which rule wins (jurisdiction > department > global; published > draft; newer effective date; “Superseded By”), configurable in a priority matrix. Step 6: Explainability – Return a structured report with citations and recommended resolution. APIs & App Host a minimal Teams app or Azure Web App UI. Use AAD for auth; enforce SharePoint permissions at retrieval time. Pros: Highly customizable, excellent for nuanced rule logic. Cons: Requires engineering effort and Azure services. Phase 6 — Conflict Detection Design (Practical Heuristics) Even with AI, implement deterministic checks first: Deterministic checks Jurisdiction precedence: If two policies conflict, prefer more specific (Country > Regional > Global). Status: Published trumps Draft; Retired excluded. Supersedes: If Policy A supersedes Policy B, B’s rules don’t apply. Effective Date: When two published policies overlap, prefer later Effective Date or Version Label. LLM-assisted checks Ask the model to: Extract subject (who), action (what), conditions (when/where), constraints (limits), exceptions. Compare normalized rules across candidate policies; flag contradictions or overlaps.
